Here's a puzzle with a pretty solution. Say we have 2n balls, where n of them are red and n of them are blue. We arrange them randomly in a circle, then we choose a ball to start at, where we'll begin to walk clockwise. At each step, we keep track of the total number of red and blue balls we've seen so far, and we stop walking immediately if we've encountered more blue balls than red balls at any point of time. (For instance, if we start at a blue ball, then we must immediately stop.) Is it always possible to pick a ball to start at where we can complete a full round? A beautiful solution.

The Cauchy–Binet formula can be thought of as a generalization of the classic identity det(AB) = det(A)det(B) to nonsquare matrices. It has some elegant proofs, and in this article I present two of them. I did not write about the most elegant one, however — the proof that uses the Lindström–Gessel–Viennot lemma, which provides a combinatorial interpretation for the determinant as counting systems of non-intersecting paths on the lattice. Do check out Martin Aigner and Günter M. Ziegler's book "Proofs from THE BOOK" for a proof, alongside all sorts of mathematical goodies.
